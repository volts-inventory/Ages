## Q92 — Hydrologic cycle: evaporation → vapour transport → precipitation
**Status:** Decided
**Context:** Pre-Q92 the planet had two parallel water stories
that didn't talk to each other:

- `water_depth` — the surface column tracked in `PhysicsState`,
  redistributed by `GravityFlow`. Set at world-gen and sloshes
  around with gravity, but never grows or shrinks.
- `Substance::Water` ↔ `Substance::Vapour` transitions in
  `Chemistry`, working on a *separate* per-cell substance density.
  The transitions correctly captured per-substrate phase
  thresholds (Q83/Q85) but had no horizontal transport — vapour
  created over hot cells condensed right back at the same cell
  with no spatial redistribution. A no-op cycle in practice.

Real planets evaporate from oceans, ride atmospheric circulation
over land, and precipitate as rain/snow that runs back to the
sea. Q91 supplied the velocity field; Q92 closes the loop.

**Decision:** Add a `Hydrology` law that runs every macro-step
after Wind, before HeatConduction. Three ordered passes:

1. **Surface evaporation:** cells with `water_depth > 0` and
   `T > boil_threshold` move
   `min(evap_rate · (T - boil) · water_depth · dt, water_depth)`
   from `water_depth` into `Substance::Vapour`. Rate scales with
   temperature excess and available water — capped so no cell
   goes negative.
2. **Vapour advection:** pair-flux upwind transport of
   `Substance::Vapour` along Q91's `(v_q, v_r)`. Same scheme
   `Wind` uses for temperature; pair-flux preserves total
   bit-exactly.
3. **Precipitation:** cells with `Substance::Vapour > 0` and
   `T < boil_threshold` move
   `min(condense_rate · (boil - T) · vapour · dt, vapour)` from
   vapour into `water_depth`. Cold cells now actually fill their
   surface column instead of bookkeeping rain as a
   never-flowing substance density.

`boil_threshold` is the substrate-aware Clausius-Clapeyron
reference boil point (Q83/Q85): aqueous ~373 K, methane ~112 K,
ammonia ~240 K, etc. Per-cell pressure-dependent saturation
curves (real meteorology has evaporation below boil too) are a
deferred follow-up — they need per-cell humidity tracking and
saturation-vapour-pressure formulas; the order-of-magnitude
behaviour ("hot evaporates, cold condenses") is captured cleanly
without that machinery.

Earth-like defaults: `evap_rate = condense_rate = 0.001`
(symmetric → cycle balances at steady state),
`vapour_advect_k = 0.01` (matches `Wind::advect_k`).

**Why this respects the project goals.**
- *Physics as close to real as possible:* the hydrologic cycle
  is the dominant precipitation/runoff mechanism on every Earth-
  like world and a major climatology driver elsewhere
  (methane lakes on Titan, sulfuric-acid clouds on Venus). Q92
  lets all of it fall out of the Q90→Q91→Q92 chain instead of
  being seeded as static water layout. Inland deserts emerge
  downwind of mountain rain shadows (once Q91 winds curve
  around terrain — a follow-up). Tropical rain belts emerge
  from equator-to-pole vapour transport. None of this is
  hardcoded.
- *Least hardcoded values:* three tunable rates (evap, condense,
  advect) and one substrate-derived boil threshold per planet.
  All four have explicit physical interpretations; the boil
  threshold is *derived* from substrate properties (Q85), not
  authored per-planet.
- *Unifies parallel water stores:* before Q92 the
  `substance(Water)` accumulator and `water_depth` column were
  unrelated. Q92 routes Vapour directly back to `water_depth` so
  precipitation is genuine surface water that `GravityFlow` then
  moves downhill. The legacy `substance(Water)` Chemistry
  transitions still work for substrate models that need a
  separate "cloud water" pool, but the dominant cycle now flows
  through `water_depth`.
- *Determinism:* pair-flux preserves total vapour bit-exactly
  (verified by `vapour_advection_conserves_total_mass`); only
  state-dependent branch is `v_along > 0` (exact Q32.32
  comparison); pair iteration is canonical-order.

**Numerical care.**
- Evaporation and precipitation transfers are individually
  capped by available stock (`raw.min(stock)`) so neither field
  goes negative.
- Defensive negative-clamp on vapour after advection covers the
  pathological pair where `v_along × dt × upwind` exceeds the
  cell's stock. Under earth-like coefficients this never
  triggers (verified empirically by 24,000-tick test).
- The `evap_rate · (T - boil) · water_depth · dt` product is the
  largest hot-path multiplication. With evap_rate=0.001,
  excess≤200 K, water_depth≤1000 m, dt=1: max ~200 — safely
  inside Q32.32.

**Lives in:** `sim/physics/src/hydrology.rs` (the law),
`sim/physics/src/lib.rs` (re-export),
`sim/physics/src/orchestration.rs` (wired into the macro-step
loop after Wind, before HeatConduction;
`integrate_civ_step` takes `hydrology: Option<&Hydrology>` so
deterministic micro-tests can pass `None`),
`sim/core/src/lib.rs` (`build_laws` returns a 7-tuple including
`Hydrology::for_substrate(boil_k)`).

**Follow-ups (not blocking).**
- *Sub-boil saturation curve.* Real evaporation occurs below
  boil at a rate set by the saturation vapour pressure (a
  Clausius-Clapeyron exponential). Lands when per-cell humidity
  becomes a tracked field — currently we collapse the curve
  into a step at boil_k.
- *Per-cell pressure-aware boil.* Q91 already updates the
  per-cell `pressure` field each tick; Hydrology could feed
  each cell's pressure through `substrate_boiling_point_k` for
  per-cell altitude-aware boil. Cheap follow-up.
- *Latent heat coupling.* Real evaporation cools the surface
  water (latent heat of vaporisation); condensation warms the
  receiving cell. Chemistry already plumbs latent heat for the
  substance-pool transitions; replicating that in Hydrology
  closes the energy budget.
- *Cloud / ice particle advection.* `Substance::Ice` doesn't
  yet advect; cold-air advection of frozen particles is the
  storm/snow story.
- *Q92-aware recognition templates.* "Trade-wind rain belt",
  "rain shadow", "monsoon onset" all become recognisable now
  that wind-driven precipitation is real. Authoring lands when
  civs start accumulating precipitation observations.
