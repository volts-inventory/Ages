## Q107 — Eccentric orbits: insolation swing across the year
**Status:** Decided
**Context:** Q97 introduced seasonal insolation swing from
axial tilt — sub-solar latitude shifts hemisphere-to-hemisphere
twice a year. But every planet's orbit was *circular*: the
planet–star distance was constant, so insolation was the same
at perihelion and aphelion. Real orbits are *elliptical* with
some eccentricity. Earth's modest e ≈ 0.0167 gives ~6 % swing in
W/m² over the year (perihelion in early January is the hottest
solar input). Highly eccentric exoplanets (e ≥ 0.3) get
*asymmetric* seasons — a long cool half (slow trip near
aphelion) and a short hot half (fast swing near perihelion).
Q107 follow-ups under both Q90 and Q97 noted this.

**Decision:**

1. **`Planet::orbital_eccentricity_x100: i64`** sampled at
   worldgen. Distribution:
   - 70 %: `e ∈ [0, 0.05]` (Earth-like, near-circular)
   - 25 %: `e ∈ [0.05, 0.30]` (Mars / Mercury-like)
   - 5 %: `e ∈ [0.30, 0.60]` (highly eccentric exoplanet)
2. **Q90 `Radiation::for_planet`** now takes
   `eccentricity_x100: i64`. Per-season insolation gets
   multiplied by:
   ```
   ecc_factor(season) = 1 / (1 - e · cos(2π · season / 12))²
   ```
   - season 0 (perihelion, sub-solar at northernmost): factor
     = `1/(1-e)²`
   - season 6 (aphelion, sub-solar at southernmost): factor
     = `1/(1+e)²`
   - season 3, 9 (equinoxes): factor = 1

   The cos product `e · cos(...)` is bounded by `e < 1`, so the
   denominator stays positive. We clamp `e` to `[0, 0.95]` for
   safety.
3. **Convention coincidence**: in Q97 / Q107, season 0 is the
   N-summer perihelion (hottest both from tilt and from being
   close to the star). For an Earth-like world (e ≈ 0.02,
   tilt ≈ 23°) this slightly amplifies the N summer / S winter
   asymmetry compared to a circular orbit; for a highly eccentric
   world the eccentricity dominates and seasons become
   asymmetric in a way real Mars exhibits.

**Why this respects the project goals.**
- *Physics as close to real as possible:* Earth's
  perihelion-aphelion variation is a real climate driver
  (~6 % insolation swing); Mars's e ≈ 0.094 produces the
  ~30 % swing that gives Mars its long cold winters and
  short hot summers. Q107 makes those qualitative differences
  emerge from one numeric field.
- *Asymmetric-season recognition:* civs on highly-eccentric
  worlds will see climate templates that don't match the
  tilt-only seasonal signature. New form vocabulary tags
  could distinguish "tilt-driven" from "eccentricity-driven"
  seasonality.
- *Determinism:* eccentricity is sampled at worldgen and never
  changes; per-season factor pre-computed once at planet
  build alongside the rest of the radiation table.

**Numerical care.**
- `1 - e·cos(...)` for `e ≤ 0.95`, `|cos| ≤ 1` stays in
  `[0.05, 1.95]`; the squared inverse is bounded by 400 (at
  e = 0.95, cos = 1). For typical Earth-like e = 0.02 the
  factor varies in `[0.96², 1.04²] = [0.92, 1.08]` — small.
- Defensive `safe_denom` clamp to 0.05 if the cos input
  pushes `1 - e·cos` non-positive (only possible at e ≥ 1,
  which we already disallow via `clamp(0, 95)`).
- Per-season cost: one cos, one division, one squaring —
  negligible alongside the existing exp/ln in the per-row
  table build.

**Lives in:** `sim/world/src/lib.rs` (new
`orbital_eccentricity_x100` field + worldgen sampler),
`sim/physics/src/radiation.rs` (new `eccentricity_x100`
parameter on `for_planet` + per-season `ecc_factor` applied
to absorbed insolation; 2 new tests),
`sim/core/src/lib.rs` (`build_laws` threads
`planet.orbital_eccentricity_x100`),
`sim/civ/src/catastrophe.rs` (test fixture).

**Follow-ups (not blocking).**
- *Eccentricity-aware recognition templates.* Templates for
  "long cool season" / "short hot season" that fire on the
  eccentricity-driven asymmetry. Lands when civs accumulate
  observations that distinguish circular-orbit seasons from
  eccentric-orbit ones.
- *Kepler's second law (varying angular velocity).* Real
  eccentric orbits sweep faster near perihelion than aphelion;
  our season-index advances at constant rate. Lands when
  per-season time-spent becomes a tracked field. For now the
  insolation peak is shorter at perihelion (just the cos²
  shape) but the *time spent* in each season is uniform.
- *Tidal heating from eccentric orbits.* Highly-eccentric
  moons / planets dissipate orbital energy as tidal flexing
  heat. Mars-Phobos, Io-Jupiter. Lands when interior-
  temperature tracking becomes a Q-decision.
