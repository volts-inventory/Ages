## Q43 — Time-stepping strategy
**Status:** Decided
**Context:** Physics needs a time-stepping scheme. Civ-sim tick is
one sim-year; physics dt is much smaller. Five strategies audited (A
fixed, B adaptive, C operator splitting, D implicit, E hierarchical),
plus two phasings within them (A1 = fixed in M1a then split in M1b,
C1 = split from M1a).
**Decision:** **C1 — operator splitting from M1a.** Each law family
has its own `integrate(state, dt)` function with its own dt; the
orchestration loop calls them at configurable sub-step ratios. CFL-
binding phenomenon (fluids) gets the finest dt; heat / chemistry /
EM each get appropriately coarser dt where their CFL bounds permit.

**Spec:**
- Per-law-family `integrate(state, dt)` function. Each family
  exposes its own CFL-derived dt.
- **Macro-step** = one sim-day (sufficient resolution for daily
  thermal cycles, divides civ-sim tick cleanly into ~365 macro-
  steps per sim-year).
- **Sub-step ratios** (defaults, configurable):
  - Fluid: ~50 sub-steps per sim-day (~30-min dt, CFL-bound by
    shallow-water wave speeds at hex-cell resolution)
  - Heat: ~1 sub-step per sim-day
  - Mechanics: applied as gravitational force at each fluid sub-
    step (no separate integration)
  - Chemistry (M1b): ~1 sub-step per ~7 sim-days (most reactions
    don't change meaningfully on minute timescales)
  - EM (M1b): ~1 sub-step per sim-day
- **Splitting scheme**: Lie splitting (sequential, first-order) for
  M1a simplicity. Strang splitting (sandwiched, second-order) is an
  escape-hatch upgrade if accuracy artefacts appear in long runs.
- **Coupling order** (Lie, sequential within a macro-step):
  1. Fluid sub-steps integrate first; gravity applied each sub-step
  2. Heat integrates once per macro-step, sees fluid velocity from
     end of fluid sub-stepping for advection
  3. (M1b) Chemistry sees post-heat state; EM sees post-fluid charge
- **Determinism**: fixed sub-step counts, fixed coupling order,
  fixed dt per family. No state-dependent branching in the
  orchestration. Same as Q36 / Q42 — deterministic by construction.

**Why C1 over A1**: heat oversolves badly under fixed-dt (CFL bound
~5×10⁹ seconds vs the ~30-min fluid dt — 170× wasted compute on
heat). Same problem multiplies in M1b for chemistry/EM. The 1-day
of design work upfront avoids a retrofit later, and the perf gain
applies from M1a.

**Why not B (adaptive dt)**: state-dependent dt selection threatens
determinism; the perf win doesn't justify the risk.

**Why not D (implicit)**: requires linear-system solvers per step;
substantially more code; M3+-grade work; not justified.

**Note to revisit:** Strang splitting (second-order accurate) is the
upgrade path if Lie's first-order splitting error accumulates
visibly over long runs. Watch for this during M1a/M1b validation;
upgrade if needed. Also revisit sub-step ratios per family if
calibration during M1a shows different CFL bounds than estimated.
