## Q110 — Boris pusher: implicit-symplectic rotation for Lorentz / Coriolis
**Status:** Decided
**Context:** Q98 Lorentz and Q106 Coriolis both apply
rotational kicks to wind velocity:

```text
Δv_q = +s · v_r
Δv_r = -s · v_q
```

This is explicit Euler. After one tick:
`|v|² → (1 + s²) · |v|²` — grows by factor `(1 + s²)` every
tick. For typical `s ≤ 0.025` this is a 0.06 %/tick growth,
slow but unbounded; over a 24,000-tick run it compounds to
~4× growth. The pre-Q110 stability ceiling for `s` was ~0.5
(rotation per tick must stay well under 90°). Both Q98 and
Q106 docs flagged: "Boris pusher would conserve `|v|` exactly."

**Decision:** Replace explicit Euler with the *exact 2D
rotation* in both `Lorentz::integrate` and
`Coriolis::integrate`:

```text
θ        = signed rotation angle for this tick
v_q_new  = +cos(θ) · v_q + sin(θ) · v_r
v_r_new  = -sin(θ) · v_q + cos(θ) · v_r
```

`cos² + sin² = 1` makes the rotation matrix orthogonal, so
`|v|² = v_q² + v_r²` is preserved exactly regardless of θ
magnitude. No stability ceiling — the rotation works for any
size step. (Strictly, the *exact* Boris algorithm decomposes
the kick into a half-step E push, B rotation, and another
half-step E push for combined E+B fields. For our pure-B / pure-
rotation case it collapses to a single direct rotation.)

**Why this respects the project goals.**
- *Physics as close to real as possible:* magnetic Lorentz
  and Coriolis forces both perform work-free rotations on
  velocity in the real world. Explicit Euler violated that
  energy invariant; Q110 restores it. Long-running plasma /
  geostrophic dynamics now converge to physically correct
  steady states instead of slowly drifting toward higher |v|.
- *Lifts the stability ceiling:* Q106 Coriolis previously had
  to absorb the huge real `2·Ω` factor into a small `coriolis_k`
  to stay within explicit-Euler stability. With Boris, the
  `coriolis_k` *could* be cranked toward physical magnitudes
  (the upper bound is now sin/cos truncation error, not
  rotational instability). Q106's quantitative-vs-qualitative
  caveat softens significantly.
- *Reuses Q101 trig:* sin and cos in Q32.32 already shipped;
  Q110 just calls them per kick. Per-cell cost is two trig
  evaluations + four multiplications + one subtraction — about
  4× the explicit-Euler hot-path cost, but bounded.
- *Determinism:* trig is deterministic in Q32.32; the rotation
  is pure arithmetic with no state-dependent branching.

**Numerical care.**
- sin/cos in Q32.32 have ~1e-7 truncation error; each
  rotation accumulates a small drift in `|v|`. Over 1000
  ticks at aggressive `lorentz_k = 0.01` (verified by
  `q110_boris_pusher_preserves_velocity_magnitude`), drift is
  bounded under 10 % — vastly better than explicit Euler's
  ~e^(s²·1000) ≈ several-orders-of-magnitude growth at the
  same coefficient.
- For typical `coriolis_k = 0.001` and earth-day rotation,
  `sin(θ) ≈ θ` and `cos(θ) ≈ 1`, so the rotation effectively
  reduces to the original explicit-Euler form for small
  steps. Boris is strictly better-than-equal to Euler at
  every coefficient.

**Lives in:** `sim/physics/src/lorentz.rs` (replaced explicit-
Euler kick with cos/sin rotation; new conservation test
`q110_boris_pusher_preserves_velocity_magnitude`),
`sim/physics/src/coriolis.rs` (same swap).

**Follow-ups (not blocking).**
- *Crank up Q106 `coriolis_k` toward physical magnitudes.*
  With Boris stability, the small-coefficient calibration
  could be revisited. Physical Earth Coriolis at 1-day step
  would give θ ≈ 12 rad/tick — 2π wraps mean the same as θ ≈
  -0.6 rad/tick. The exact rotation handles this fine, but
  sub-tick interpretability matters for the recognition layer.
- *Re-introduce E-field push.* When the simulation grows a real
  electric-field forcing (different from the static Lorentz on
  charge motion), the full Boris half-step-push / B-rotate /
  half-step-push needs the complete algorithm, not just the
  rotation core.
- *Benchmark vs explicit Euler.* Current Q110 hot-path is ~4×
  the cost of pre-Q110 Euler. Ought to be still negligible at
  our cell counts but worth profiling if cell-count grows
  significantly.
